Bad Advice Parents and Teachers Give About Studying Computer Science

This blog post is for people thinking about studying computer science, engineering, programming, or getting in to a career with computing. I'm going to dispel some myths dumbass parents spread, and share some personal opinions. I studied a PhD in Computer Science, and an honours engineering degree. I worked in academia teaching these subjects, so I've seen behind the curtain. I now work in the tech/engineering industry in AR (augmented reality) / 3D graphics - one of the hot areas in computers right now.

My parents and teachers were mostly from the Baby Boomers generation. The rules were very different for that generation. You didn't need a degree to get in to a profession. You could buy your own cars (plural) working side jobs after school/college. Holding a post-doctoral degree was extremely rare. You could have a mortgage and buy a house working a 9-5 job with no entry requirements. There were more apprenticeships and internship programs. University life was heavily subsidised, and you could actually be paid to study (still true in some countries).

Very few of these people owned their own computer (still very expensive and unfashionable), but most were aware of large mainframes being installed in corporate head offices, and the highly specialised work required to program them.

The perceptions of our parents and teachers, both in terms of the job market, and in terms of the details of tech jobs, were woefully out of date, and didn't anticipate the boom to come. We received some astonishingly bad advice. This hasn't changed much for the current generation, except that all those people have Facebook accounts now.

"You shouldn't - too much competition from super-smart people, study arts/law/ instead"

FALSE - on both counts - maybe that was true once, in 1970. Before Unix. We can not find enough people, and the jobs keep growing, in new and interesting areas that didn't exist before. Most companies will (at least try to) have a range of employee experience levels. Some only hire the "best of the best of the best" and have a very small group - that's fine, but not a good reflection of the industry - most work doesn't need that level of expense, and the barriers are lower because we have better tools.

"My child is very introverted, doesn't have great people skills, likes playing video games and therefore should study computers/engineering"

MISINFORMED - Nobody works alone in a cupboard. Those few that do work solo need to be incredibly charming self-promoters and organisers. Good communication and people skills are absolutely vital to making projects work well. We have too many people with this impression, and they often don't get through the first year of engineering because it turns out they just wanted to play video games, or they don't want to work with others, write clearly, or present in front of their peers. You're always going to have introverted people on your team, and they are often great, but we are - in my opinion - a bit imbalanced culturally here, and it can often get a bit messy (read: very expensive to fix) when work isn't communicated clearly. I think if you're genuinely interested in working in this area and you know you may have problems with confidence or working with others, then it's something that needs to be improved through practice, training, and goal-setting.

"You need to be a maths whiz to work in computers"

FALSE - the overwhelming majority of computer programming work is either working with logic "if this is true, then this should happen, but not if this other thing is true" or project planning and communication. On a daily basis most mathematics is very basic addition, multiplication, maybe some simple angle calculations. You don't need the calculus to make websites. Specific domains require specific mathematical tools, so it won't hurt to also be good at mathematics, but you'll probably be learning new areas, or re-learning linear algebra anyway. Most of my students in computer graphics had a linear algebra theory course (vectors, matrices) the year before my course, but pretty much had to learn it again to use it in a practical application. Weird right?

"With a [bachelor's] degree in computers/engineering you'll name your salary"

VERY FALSE - maybe during the Dot-com boom. Everybody has a bachelors degree now. Everybody. The front-desk receptionist will have a bachelor's degree in marketing. My honest opinion is that this is a bit good to raise education, but also a bit of a (read: unbelievably enormous) scam encouraged by universities, governments, and the banks organising student loans. I'm sorry but in most countries the cost of education no longer justifies the salary you'll get afterwards unless you're really at the peak of your game - do some reading on the accumulated cost of a four-year degree, add a year just in case, factor in compound interest from student loans, and do some Glassdoor research on junior engineer salaries in your area. Consider that the Masters degree is the new Bachelors. Most graduates will be in their forties by the time they can pay this off. It's a bit better in tech, but we're talking thirties. Unless you live in the Nordic countries. If you or your children can move to Europe, get residency, and study on local rates - seriously consider this. Seriously. Then it's an easy win financially. I paid my loan off in my early thirties only because I had a side business selling tutorial books that surprised me with becoming super popular, not because of my day job.

Now, the second problem is getting in - you'll have extreme difficulty getting your foot in the door to a job with only a degree. Some places will hire grads, but some of these places are also set up to under-pay them or burn them out. There are some very manipulative tech employers out there. Grads have to be very very switched on with getting internships, figuring out the expectations of employers in advance, preparing some sort of portfolio work to show or other evidence of teamwork and depth of knowledge. If you're in, and you have some mobility in moving city/country etc then your options open up a lot.

"You need to study computer science to learn computer programming".

SORT-OF - I went this way and very much enjoyed it, but you can also teach yourself. Having a head-start on college isn't a bad idea. Ideally you find an experienced mentor to guide you and introduce you to right subjects. I think it's a real shame that home computers, despite everyone having one now, no longer ship with programming tools or manuals. The good news is that on-line resources are boundless. Knowing where to start is still tough. I suggest buying a couple of "Beginner's Teach Yourself Whatever Language" type of books to play around and get a feel for things. They will tell your what tools to get, what some of the basic concepts are and so on. Then you'll get a feeling for which websites to go to. I don't think it matters where you start, as long as it's fun, and you find out what you don't like - maybe it's not for you at all. What you'll be missing out on if you don't study at university - meeting interesting and valuable people, friendly rivalry, goal-based evaluations, not going too far off on crazy tangents, some great and some dreadful teachers, huge fees.

Parents and Teachers

Please stop giving kids this kind of advice - buy them a "Teach Yourself Python/JavaScript/C/Go/Spanish/Other" book that looks a little bit fun and quite easy to read over an afternoon. This section is in your bookshop. They'll get the idea. Maybe try it yourself.
